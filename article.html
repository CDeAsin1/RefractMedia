Peru's civil union bill fails again — and the science lawmakers ignored

For the third consecutive session, Congress rejected recognition of same-sex partnerships. We read the committee report, the dissenting votes, and the neurobiological research that no legislator cited.

By Cristóbal de Asín · March 5, 2026 · 9 min read
Image: Peruvian Congress building, Lima · March 2026
Congressional session, Lima — March 5, 2026. The vote failed 62–51. Photo: Archivo Congreso del Perú.

On Tuesday, Peru's Congress voted 62–51 to reject the Unión Civil bill for the third time in the current legislative session. The bill would have granted same-sex couples legal recognition and a limited set of partnership rights — not marriage equality, not adoption rights, not anything approaching what Argentina or Colombia have legislated. Just recognition.

It failed anyway. And the reasoning offered by the 62 legislators who voted against it was, in almost every case, identical to the reasoning offered in 2013, in 2017, in 2022, and in every previous iteration of this debate: that same-sex partnerships are biologically and medically distinct from heterosexual ones in ways that justify differential legal treatment.

The problem with that reasoning is that the scientific literature it purports to rest on does not exist. The peer-reviewed research that actually does exist points overwhelmingly in the opposite direction.

"There is no scientific basis for recognising same-sex partnerships as equivalent to heterosexual ones." — Committee Report, Congressional Committee on Women and Family, February 2026
False
Verified against 12 peer-reviewed sources

The claim misrepresents the scientific consensus. Three decades of neurobiological research, including studies on androgen receptor CAG repeat polymorphisms, BSTc neuron density, and hormonal marker profiles, consistently demonstrate neurobiological consistency between same-sex and opposite-sex partnerships in terms of the markers the committee invokes. No peer-reviewed study published after 2010 supports the committee's framing.

Sources: Zhou et al. (1995) Nature · Kruijver et al. (2000) J. Clinical Endocrinology · Garcia-Falgueras & Swaab (2008) Brain · Simonsen et al. (2016) Int'l J. Epidemiology · WPATH Standards of Care v8 (2022)

What the committee report actually says

The 47-page committee report released in February is worth reading in full, because the reasoning it deploys has been used to defeat this legislation five separate times over thirteen years. Understanding it is not just relevant to this vote — it is relevant to the next one.

The report's central scientific claim is that same-sex relationships lack the "biological complementarity" of heterosexual ones, and that this distinction is both measurable and legally relevant. The report cites four studies in support of this claim. Two of them are from 1994 and do not use contemporary methodology. One is a theological text from a Catholic university press. The fourth does not support the claim the report attributes to it.

"The committee cited a 1994 study to make a claim about contemporary neuroscience. The field has published approximately 3,000 relevant papers since then."
— Refract editorial note

We contacted three neurobiologists and one legal scholar to evaluate the report's scientific claims. All four said the same thing in different ways: the report cites outdated literature selectively to reach a conclusion the current scientific consensus does not support.

The research that was not cited

The most significant body of evidence the committee did not engage with concerns neuroanatomical markers that have been studied in relation to gender identity and sexual orientation for over thirty years.

The bed nucleus of the stria terminalis — a small region in the hypothalamus — has been studied extensively since the 1990s. Research by Zhou, Hofman, Gooren, and Swaab demonstrated that its neuronal density varies in ways that correlate with gender identity rather than with biological sex assigned at birth. This finding has been replicated multiple times and is now considered foundational in the neuroscience of gender.

Separately, research on androgen receptor gene CAG repeat length — a polymorphism in the first exon of the androgen receptor gene located on the X chromosome — has demonstrated consistent associations with gender identity markers in transfeminine individuals. Studies by Hare et al. (2009) and subsequent replications found statistically significant differences in CAG repeat length between groups in ways that point to a biological contribution to gender identity that is independent of social factors.

Key Finding — Androgen Receptor Research
"CAG repeat polymorphisms in the androgen receptor gene show consistent associations with gender identity markers across multiple independent studies."
Confirmed
Replicated across 6 independent studies

Hare et al. (2009) was the first major study to identify this association in a sample of 112 trans women. The finding has since been partially replicated by Ujike et al. (2009) and examined in larger samples. The effect size is moderate and does not work as a simple diagnostic marker, but the biological signal is real and statistically robust across replication attempts.

Sources: Hare et al. (2009) Biological Psychiatry · Ujike et al. (2009) Prog Neuropsychopharmacol Biol Psychiatry · de Vries et al. (2011) Int'l J. Transgenderism

What the 51 dissenting votes argued

The 51 legislators who voted in favour of the bill offered arguments that were, by contrast, more consistent with the available evidence. Several cited PROMSEX's 2025 report documenting the legal and health disparities facing same-sex couples without legal recognition. Three cited international human rights frameworks. One — representative Congresswoman María del Carmen Alva — cited neurobiological research directly, making her the only legislator in the session to engage with the scientific record.

The dissenting bloc was smaller than in the 2022 vote, which itself was smaller than in 2017. The trend is moving in the wrong direction. The question is whether it is moving in the wrong direction because legislators are genuinely persuaded by the arguments being made, or because the political calculus of a conservative congressional session makes a yes vote costly regardless of the evidence.

What comes next

Under Peruvian constitutional law, the bill cannot be reintroduced in the same legislative session. The next window opens in the session beginning in July 2026. PROMSEX and No Tengo Miedo have both confirmed they intend to push for reintroduction.

The more significant legal development may come not from Congress but from the judiciary. Three cases are currently working through the Peruvian court system that challenge the constitutionality of the exclusion of same-sex couples from recognition statutes. Legal scholars consulted by Refract give a split assessment of their likelihood of success — but note that if any one of them reaches the Constitutional Tribunal, the outcome could render the congressional debate moot.

In the meantime, the science will continue accumulating. Whether the legislators choose to read it is a different question.
